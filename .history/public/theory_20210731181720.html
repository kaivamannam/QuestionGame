In this theory guide, you will learn about Bloom’s taxonomy and Depth of Knowledge. After reading this guide, you would be able to identify higher-order (HOTS) questions from lower-order (LOTS) questions, and you will be able to identify questions that need a greater level of knowledge to answer them.

You do not need to memorise anything here but understand the concepts below.

Bloom's Taxonomy

In simple terms, a taxonomy means a classification system. Benjamin Bloom created a hierarchical model with six levels of complexity. The higher the level, the tougher it is to think about it and come up with answers. The sequence of cognitive skills are Remembering, Understanding, Applying, Analysing, Evaluating, and Creating.

The upper three levels (Create, Evaluate and Analyse) form the HOTS (Higher Order Thinking Skills). The bottom three levels(Remember, Understand and Apply) form the LOTS (Lower Order Thinking Skills). You should develop the ability to generate questions based on HOTS levels.

So what are these levels exactly? Let me explain these levels using examples.

1. The first level of Bloom’s Taxonomy is to Remember. Activities such as memorising a poem, recalling state capitals, remembering math formulas form the first level.

Example phrases: What is _____?, Define the term ___?, How would you identify ___?, List the factors that ___?, Name the ___?, When did the ___?

2. The second level of Bloom’s Taxonomy is to Understand. Examples include making a list of people based on their characteristics, illustrate the difference between a rectangle and a square, or even summarize a story or a speech.

Example phrases: What facts or ideas show ___?, Write a summary of___?, Provide an example of ___?, Classify the following ___?

3. The third level of Bloom’s Taxonomy is to Apply. Examples include using a formula to solve a math problem, choosing a design to build a new place or revising parts of speech or text to achieve a purpose.

Example phrases: What approach would you use to ___?, Can you apply the method used to other instances ___?, List the principles using which _____?, Do you know another instance where ___?

4. The fourth level of Bloom’s Taxonomy is to Analyze. Examples include identify the ‘parts of’ democracy, explain how the steps of the scientific process work together, identify why a machine isn’t working

Example phrases: What approach would you use to ___?, Can you apply the method used to other instances ___?, List the principles using which _____?, Do you know another instance where ___?

5. The fifth level of Bloom’s Taxonomy is to Evaluate. Examples include making a judgment regarding an ethical dilemma, interpreting the significance of a given law of physics, or illustrate the relative value of technological innovation in a specific setting.

6. The sixth and highest level of Bloom’s taxonomy is to Create. Examples include creating a new design to solve an ‘old’ problem, delete the least useful arguments in a persuasive essay, or write a poem or essay based on a given theme and tone.

That’s a lot of information. Please don’t worry about memorising them. Just remember, the higher the level, the more challenging the task or questions becomes. And that we have to target to develop questions based on the final three levels (Analyse, Evaluate and Create).




Depth of Knowledge

Norman Webb created another classification model with four levels based on the amount of knowledge and knowledge processing required to answer a question. Higher the level, the greater the challenge.

Level 1. Recall and Reproduction: questions at this level require recall of facts or rote application of simple procedures. The task does not require any cognitive effort beyond remembering the right response or formula.

Level 2. Skills and Concepts: At this level, a student must make some decisions about his or her approach. Tasks with more than one mental step, such as comparing, organizing, summarizing, predicting, and estimating, are usually Level 2.

Level 3. Strategic Thinking: At this level of complexity, students must use planning and evidence, and thinking is more abstract. A task with multiple valid responses, where students must justify their choices, would be Level 3. Examples include solving non-routine problems, designing an experiment, or analyzing characteristics of a genre.

Level 4. Extended Thinking: Level 4 tasks require the most complex cognitive effort. Students synthesize information from multiple sources, often over an extended period, or transfer knowledge from one domain to solve problems in another. Designing a survey and interpreting the results, analyzing multiple texts to extract themes, or writing an original story in a specific style would all be examples of Level 4.
